          This thread has been massively derailed, so I am going to close it, but I'll explain why Let's Plays are legal and TIHYDP are not, per my legal consultation.

          Let's Plays/video game playthroughs are legal because they are transformative works in the utmost sense: if you just boot up a game, you'll never get passed the title screen without your interaction and input. Everything you do while playing the game - every input, every button, etc. is YOUR unique take and way to play the game. Commentary, facecam and the like only add to this. Many people have argued that "you only pay for a license to play the game, not to OWN the game and therefore Let's Plays aren't legal" but that's only true in one sense: you DO NOT own the game, but you own the right to play it and CREATE with it. Therefore, any GAMEPLAY is legally your property, but the game itself is not.

          For example: if you buy a musical instrument, you have every right to play that instrument in the way YOU see fit. You can record the music you create and sell it, and it is 100% your own, with NO ownership reverting to the company that designed, patented and manufactured that instrument. Video games are the same: you pay to play them, and that gameplay becomes your "music." You can argue that the company that designed the game created the groundwork for your gameplay, but that is NOT the same as OWNING the gameplay; much like you can say the instrument manufacturer created the groundwork for the music you make with that instrument. Because of the INTERACTIVITY, that music/gameplay/whatever becomes YOUR legal property to share and make money with, in any way you see fit. What is NOT allowed, would be to completely duplicate that musical instrument, or to copy/distribute the code of a video game as THAT would violate trademark/copyright patents. But if you've LEGALLY purchased the instrument/game, you can do whatever you want with it as your interaction with that thing created a transformative work that is your own. You simply cannot REPRODUCE and sell the original product.

           

          NOW, as for TIHYDP...

          First off, nothing is transformative. These videos just take raw gameplay from my playthroughs, chop them up, and reupload them with supposed "funny because they're so insulting" comments and the like. That's a FAR stretch from actually playing a musical instrument, or creating a playthrough by playing a game and adding commentary. So any argument that it's "transformative" is bunk from a legal standpoint.

          Next, no permission was ever given to use the video footage. ALL videos in TIHYDP are illegally downloaded from YouTube, violating my personal copyrights AND my advertisement agreement with Machinima (so it's actually two crimes in one). It's not like where you BOUGHT the instrument first, or purchased the game license; it's blatant theft. 

          Third, the argument that they are "commentary/criticism/review" is also false. Legally, TIHYDP is considered SLANDER because it only portrays one side or aspect of the entire entity, and in a PURPOSEFULLY harmful/negative light. It would be like if I did a review of Batman vs. Superman, and used video clips, but I ONLY talked about the things I disliked/the movie did poorly and then said "this movie sucks monkey shit, don't see it." If a reviewer who had any kind of legitimate reach did that, they could EASILY be sued for misrepresentation/slander, and that's literally what TIHYDP is, at its core. 

           

          So why are TIHYDPs allowed to exist? It's simple: because YouTube's copyright takedown system is to protect THEM, not content creators. I was told by legal counsel that in order to take down a TIHYDP video on YouTube permanently, I'd have to file an individual lawsuit (which costs hundreds if not thousands of dollars) for EACH INDIVIDUAL VIDEO. As you know, there are quite a bunch of them out there, and I'm not rich, so this is impossible. The only thing I have the power to do is file a copyright claim against the video (which is 100% legal and I am in the right to do), but the person who uploaded it can simply dispute the claim. If I don't legally file a lawsuit within 2 weeks of their dispute, the video will go back live no matter what, EVEN THOUGH I'M LEGALLY IN THE RIGHT. As I've said TONS of times, until someone DIRECTLY SUES YOUTUBE for allowing mass copyright infringement to happen on their site due to their insane lack of care for US Copyright law, they'll continue to employ this broken system that actually SUPPORTS theft, and promotes the mass filing of false claims for malicious purposes.

          In reality? I COULD legally prove that TIHYDP has hurt my personal life, my business, my online persona and that I'll be damaged for the rest of my life because of them. All it takes is a Google search. And I could sue YouTube for allowing the videos, and probably win in court, maybe up to a few hundred thousand bucks (estimated, could be more or less depending on how trial goes etc.). But undoubtedly that'd be it for me. I'd have to leave YouTube the second I filed the lawsuit and pursue other avenues of employment. Right now, I'm happy doing this as a job and have no plans to stop. So until it comes to that point, I'll just "sit on it" and keep doing what I'm doing.

           

          Now, thread closed.
