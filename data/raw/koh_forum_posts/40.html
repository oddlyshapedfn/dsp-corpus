          From my experience, it's a combination of a LOT of stuff.

          -Timing (striking when the iron is hot)
          -The title of the video (aka "clickbait")
          -The Thumbnail of the video
          -Proper tagging of a video
          -Word of mouth (FUCKING HUGE in my experience)
          -Random factors regarding YouTube's search engine

           

          Case in point: Last month, I played the DOOM multiplayer beta. The stream didn't get many views at all; but because I Played it the night it launched, part 1 of my video series somehow showed up as THE top video in search, regarding the beta on YouTube. The video got over 50k views in 3 days, but none of the other videos in that series showed up in YouTube search the same way, so the views declined sharply over the course of the playlist. In addition, nothing CRAZY interesting happens in the video (it's my first match, so I kind of suck at it) and therefore it didn't get much word of mouth interest.

          Now let's talk Homefront: people have been speculating for years how it would turn out. When it released, TONS of people wanted to know if it was good before they bought it (not always the case with other overhyped AAA releases, for example). So people were flocking to YouTube within 1-2 days to see if the game was any good. Imagine if you were searching for reviews and found a video that, in the title, said it was the WORST game someone had ever played? Probably gotta watch that one, right? And luckily, unlike a lot of other people, I had tons of supplementary info in the review (history of Homefront, and comparing it to other bad games I've played) which most people don't include. 

          In the first day, the video didn't get many views AT ALL. Maybe 3-5k which is typical for a video on KOGaming. But THEN, for whatever reason, it showed up in YouTube search results (much like the DOOM video) and BOOM! It had an entirely new audience. Word of mouth has now allowed it to go crazy viral and it'll probably keep getting 10-20k views a day for 2-3 more days, then finally calm down.

          The good thing is: KOGaming has gotten almost 3k new subs in the past few days because of the video, and my other reviews have seen a sharp spike in interest. Today I released the Far Harbor review, and later this week I'll have Overwatch coverage. Even if nothing else goes viral on the channel, it'll at the very least see increased view numbers for a while. So it's all good.

          Adblock is not theft.

          in Other

          Posted May 23, 2016

          Per dictionary.com:

          theft

          the act of stealing; the wrongful taking and carrying away of the personal goods or property of another; larceny.

           

          Although theft can take many forms, the emphasis of the definition here is "wrongful."

          When a full-time YouTuber produces content and monetizes it, that creates a contract with both YouTube and the viewer. You may watch said content, for free, as long as you also watch the advertisement attached to it. If you did not watch that advertisement, you are taking deservedly earned revenue out of the pocket of the content creator, and that is both legally and morally wrong - i.e. "wrongful taking" and therefore, per dictionary.com, considered theft.

          The majority of higher-quality content on the internet is free to view, because of advertisement revenue. This is a long-established model for many sources of information and entertainment; broadcast television is another very popular one.

          Let's take television as a great example: people have bypassed commercials in various ways in the last decade (recording/DVR, on-demand video etc.) and nobody has been held accountable, nor prosecuted for it. And look at what's happened: the emergence of pay-to-watch services such as Netflix, Hulu, Amazon and the like. Television with advertisements is a dying medium because people found ways around watching the advertisements, and many shows may ONLY now be watched if 1. you subscribe to one of those on-demand services or 2. you purchase the DVD/blu-ray set after the season is over. Television is, very much so, turning into a "pay-to-watch" medium BECAUSE people are bypassing all the advertisements that made it free.

           

          So, if you want YouTube to also become a mandatory paid subscription (i.e. everyone has to get "YouTube Red," per se) then by all means, continue to use adblock on the site and steal to your heart's content. You will be first in line to whine, complain and throw a temper tantrum when you can no longer watch ANYTHING on the internet for free, and instead have to pay for it all, because of your shortsighted greed.

          I've never used adblock and I never will. It's incredibly annoying, especially for those "clickbait" sites that spam a ridiculous amount of ads in your face. But you'd never be able to use the vast majority of the internet if those advertisements didn't exist. Mature, intelligent, logical adults understand this. Sadly, the vast majority of internet users are NOT those kinds of people.

          Scoring: been explained before. Weight of tons of factors: gameplay (quality and quantity), art/graphical design, story, replayability, cost, etc. Then weighing that with fun factor (a game could have tons of content, but it's all boring) and comparison to other games of the genre, franchise as well as other games available for sale at the time. Quarter points allow me to differentiate between games that may be close in quality but have small factors setting them apart. Giving two games an 8 when I slightly liked one more than the other wouldn't be fair. Lots of reviewers use too broad a scale, leading to their scores not being scaled properly (I really liked this game but it's a 7.5, but this game sucked and it's a 7, for example; I've seen this TONS of times at IGN, sadly). The actual number scoring is just something I picked up when I started and doesn't really have a purpose behind it. Honestly, if people just watched my reviews and ignored the scores, they'd be just as good; sadly, people have short attention spans and demand numbers.

           

          Comparisons: are VERY necessary. I'm not just reviewing a game for me; I'm reviewing it for the common gamer/consumer. If you can only buy one game this month, and I review 4, I want you to know which one, overall, is the best value for your dollar. If I didn't consider other games currently available, this would be hugely unfair. For example: although many people might actually LOVE the gameplay of StarFox Zero, considering we've had tons of games lately with great gameplay and about 10 times more content, it would be ridiculously irresponsible of me to NOT make that comparison when considering my score.
